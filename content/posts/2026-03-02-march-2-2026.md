# Dixon City Council Special Meeting Summary
## February 23 — Budget Work Session for Fiscal Year 2027

The council held a special afternoon session to kick off budget planning for the fiscal year running May 1, 2026 through April 30, 2027. Present were Council members Bishop, Considine, Oras, and Veneer, along with Mayor Hughes. City Manager Danny and department heads presented their budgets one by one.

---

### The Big Picture

The city expects to finish FY27 just **$68,000 in the black** — a razor-thin margin despite projecting **$560,000 in new revenue**, mostly from growth in the Route 88 commercial corridor. Two major cost drivers are squeezing the budget:

- **Health insurance**, up an estimated 20% through the city's joint insurance pool. Communities outside such pools are reportedly facing 45% increases.
- **Public safety pensions**, which now exceed **$3 million annually** in combined police and fire payments. Pension bond payments rise each year under the current structure, though refinancing becomes an option at year seven (around 2029-2030).

City Manager Danny credited the consolidation of downstate police and fire pension funds with significantly better investment returns — now performing at 10-12%, compared to the roughly 6.25-6.5% the city managed on its own.

### Revenue Highlights

Finance Director Becky Leslie, presenting her final budget before retiring in June, walked through key revenue lines:

- **CPPRT** (Corporate Personal Property Replacement Tax): Up $250,000, now that the state has apparently finished clawing back years of overpayments.
- **Sales tax**: Up $300,000, driven by 88 corridor growth.
- **Income tax**: Up $100,000, though the state legislature may freeze municipal distributions. Leslie said even a freeze wouldn't hurt — the budgeted figure matches what the city actually received in FY25.
- **Gaming tax**: Up $50,000.
- **Landfill revenue**: Up $50,000. Leslie flagged this as a line to watch; it depends on the garbage hauler dumping at the Lee County landfill, per the current contract. If the landfill reaches capacity, this revenue could disappear.
- **Motel tax**: Budgeted at $300,000, a figure Leslie said she wouldn't have imagined three or four years ago.
- **Property tax levy**: Zero. The city levied nothing for its general fund this year because it had to levy for Social Security ($250,000) and increased pension obligations ate the rest.

Leslie cautioned that the **General Capital Fund (Fund 31)** — which pays for squad cars, dump trucks, and equipment — has no dedicated funding source. It relies on annual "turn-ins" (surplus revenues), which have been declining. The fund is healthy now but bears watching.

### Personnel and Staffing Notes

- **Becky Leslie** retires in June; **Heather McCarter** is being groomed as her successor and presented her first budget segment.
- **Matt Heckman**, assistant city manager/public works director, retires this summer after nearly 35 years. **Matt Hewitt** will become public works director; **Cameron Magney** will take over the water department.
- A new **Human Resources department** appears in the budget for the first time, carved out of the administration budget. A one-time expense covers implementation of a records management system (UKG software).
- The police department has a placeholder for a **potential additional administrative assistant**, restoring staffing to levels of four or five years ago. This ties partly to the crushing volume of FOIA requests.
- A firefighter's potential **military deployment** starting in October could force significant overtime spending — a 41% increase in that line item is budgeted as a placeholder.

### Department-by-Department Highlights

**City Clerk/FOIA:** A $15,000 placeholder is budgeted for software to manage the flood of Freedom of Information Act requests, many believed to come from automated bots. Body camera footage redaction is particularly labor-intensive.

**Economic Development:** Flat budget. The Lee County Industrial Development Association payment was shifted here from the PR/marketing budget, which accounts for an apparent increase.

**PR & Marketing:** The Discover Dixon contract ($325,000) is up for renewal; Executive Director Amanda Wyke will present to council in March. A three-year contract extension is under consideration.

**Building & Zoning:** Up about $25,800, mostly wages and benefits. A $15,000 placeholder exists for possible new permitting/registration software.

**Fire Department (Chief Buskell):**
- Ambulance revenue projected at $1.25 million (conservative).
- A new ambulance ordered in May 2024 ($325,000) should arrive during FY27.
- The department is exploring a **joint training facility with Dixon Rural Fire**, with the city's share budgeted at roughly half the total cost. The chief declined to give a firm total, saying quotes are still being refined.
- A phased transition to **Starcom radios** begins, starting with officers. Full switchover could take two years. The better coverage will eventually eliminate the need for more expensive mobile radios in apparatus.
- New lighter-weight protective gear will extend the life of structural firefighting turnout gear.

**Police Department (Chief Evans):**
- Overall increase of 7.86% (~$360,000), with $300,000-plus of that driven by contractual obligations (salaries, insurance, dispatch contract).
- The 911 dispatch contract alone increased over $43,000.
- New investments include: forensic cell phone download software (Cellbrite, nearly doubled in cost), investigative tools (Leads Online, Nighthawk), a scheduling platform, cell phones in each squad car for photos and mobile work, and a **proposed department app** ($13,000) to connect with the community.
- Capital requests: a police side-by-side vehicle for events, a **complete Starcom radio conversion** ($410,000), and a used unmarked investigation vehicle.
- Every squad would get a **ballistic shield**; expired gas masks and filters will be replaced.
- The council was asked to weigh in on whether to continue funding a part-time **Community Service Officer** position ($18,000) that has been vacant for three to four years.

**Streets (Tyler):**
- Up 5.24% ($83,000), mostly personnel costs plus $20,000 for salt shed repairs.
- Capital highlights: $150,000 placeholder for **downtown bollards and barriers** for event safety (includes $80,000 carryover); a dump truck ($140,000 carryover, in production); continued LED streetlight conversion; completion of the **Galena Avenue streetlight project** ($130,000); and a **new street sweeper** ($360,000) to replace a 2008 unit.

**Properties:**
- Down 3.23% overall after completion of a one-time electrical project.
- Capital: two pickup truck replacements ($65,000 each — one for properties, one for cemetery); $100,000 for the **Chamber/Main Street building roof**; $140,000 for **City Hall north wall repairs** (the wall was originally built against an adjacent building and was never meant to be exposed to weather); $40,000 for Christmas decorations along the new pedestrian bridge; $50,000 for **riverfront fountain plumbing repairs**; $15,000 for replacement riverfront tables.
- The **Berlin Wall memorial** was recently vandalized with graffiti. Cost to repair is unknown because the structure itself bears painted artwork that complicates restoration. Staff is researching options.

**Cemetery:**
- Up 3.25%. The new Oakwood Cemetery building is nearing completion.
- A **columbarium** ($50,000) is proposed — an above-ground structure for cremation remains, intended to be revenue-generating over time.
- The historic **mausoleum** needs structural engineering assessment ($30,000 this year) with an estimated $300,000 in repairs projected for FY28.

**Airport (Matt Hewitt):**
- Revenues include federal/state grants, fuel sales ($45,000, offset by fuel expense), and $30,000 in rental income from farmland and hangars.
- A $20,000 general fund transfer supports the **terminal building modernization** project, which is 90% federally funded.
- Capital projects: hangar repairs ($40,000, local funds only); a new AWOS weather system (mostly federal dollars); runway 12/30 rehabilitation (carried over, going to bid); and the terminal modernization.

**Water Department (Cameron Magney):**
- A 2.5% rate increase takes effect — the first after a four-year freeze.
- Metered sales revenue is budgeted higher as post-COVID usage has recovered.
- Operating expenses up just 1.12%. Electricity is the biggest cost increase (~$30,000). Telephone costs dropped 28% after switching phone systems.
- Capital: in-house water main projects ($115,000 combined); a new hydro-excavation trailer ($40,000); filter pipe replacements at the main plant ($70,000, with more years of this expected); a truck replacement ($60,000); and **Water Tower #9 rehabilitation** ($550,000) — a full blast, repaint, and interior work expected to provide 25 years of service life. (Tower 9 is the one near Culver's.)

**Wastewater (Josh McNitt):**
- The 2.5% user fee increase resumes after a four-year freeze.
- Operating expenses up 5.85%.
- All capital items are carryovers: the major **$25 million treatment plant revamp** (construction has begun with some concrete and piping, targeting December 2027 completion but supply chain delays may push that); a new Morton storage building (similar to the Oakwood Cemetery building); the Purity Mills lift station; and a roll-off truck being outfitted at Bunnell's.
- The treatment plant project, when complete, should serve the city for 25-30 years. EPA loan forgiveness is being pursued.

### Capital Fund Concerns

The overall capital budget started at $2.2 million and was trimmed to $1.9 million. The two largest items are the police Starcom radio system ($410,000) and the street sweeper ($360,000).

Staff recommended allocating $1.5 million of this year's $2.2 million in turn-in revenue to the capital fund, with approximately $700,000 going toward a required **seawall project** to be presented Wednesday.

The city manager cautioned that turn-in revenues are trending downward — pre-COVID they sometimes dipped to $800,000-$900,000 — and the days of $3 million-plus surpluses appear over. The capital fund is healthy for the next five to six years, but staff is beginning to analyze whether longer-lived assets (like a street sweeper with a 20-year life) should be financed rather than purchased outright, spreading costs across the taxpayers who benefit from them.

### Other Notes

- The **comprehensive plan**, led by Mayor Hughes, has been completed with broad community input (surveys, public engagement, over 80 participants).
- Housing remains a top priority: workforce housing, 55+ housing, and single-family development at Washington Park (already more than half full). Craig Wilson's team is building 24-32 new apartments on Middle Road.
- The **Gateway commercial area** will soon house OSF Healthcare and Tropical Smoothie, with additional tenants not yet public.
- **Viaduct Point** (between the Peoria Avenue bridge and the new pedestrian bridge) visioning should wrap up in 2026, with development beginning in 2027. The old NAC building may come down this year.
- A **$2 million federal grant** from Congressman LaHood will fund "Bloody Gulch Park" with retention ponds, fishing, and a pathway connecting Seventh Street to the 88 corridor. Pond work begins this year.
- **Project Rock** in Page Park is ahead of schedule, set to open early summer with a grand opening tied to a **July 4th / America's 250th celebration**, led by Councilman Bishop.
- The city wants to extend its pathway system to Sauk Valley Community College, coordinating with the park district, school district, SVCC, and Lee County.
- The city confirmed its policy of **buying vehicles locally** through Ken Nelson, paying a negotiated amount above state bid pricing.

The meeting adjourned after roughly two hours with no public comment. Budget discussions continue Wednesday.
