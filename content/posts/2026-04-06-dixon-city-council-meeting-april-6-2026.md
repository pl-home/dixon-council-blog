# Dixon City Council Meeting Summary
**Regular Session — April 7, 2026**

All council members were present: Mayor Glen Hughes, and Councilmen Dennis Considine, Mary Oros, Mike Venier, and Chris Bishop.

---

## Budget Public Hearing

The council held a brief public hearing on the fiscal year 2026-27 budget. No members of the public had questions. City Manager Danny Langloss outlined two pending amendments: adding back a marked and unmarked police vehicle (cut earlier to balance the budget), and increasing the Discover Dixon contract from $325,000 to $350,000 — the first bump in three years, with an agreement that no further increases would be requested for another three years. Both changes will be incorporated before the budget gets final approval at the next meeting.

## Proclamations

Mayor Hughes read proclamations designating April as Child Abuse Prevention Month and Sexual Assault Awareness Month. Representatives from Shining Star Advocacy Center and CASA were present for the first; no one from the YWCA attended for the second.

## Discover Dixon Annual Report

Amanda (director of Discover Dixon) presented the organization's annual report, noting that its five-person staff produced more than 100 events in 2025. Key figures:

- **5.3 million digital content views**, with engagement up sharply across the board
- **$12.9 million in estimated visitor spending**, a 33.8% year-over-year increase
- More than **25,000 visitors** attended Discover Dixon events
- Overnight "leakage" — visitors spending time in Dixon but sleeping elsewhere — dropped to 13.5%, a 20.3% improvement, attributed largely to two new hotels
- Chicago remains the primary tourism market, with additional reach into the Quad Cities, Peoria, Madison, Milwaukee, and expanding into Springfield, Champaign, and Quincy
- Dixon hosted the **Illinois Main Street Conference** for the first time in 20 years
- A new mural on the Water Department building is planned for this summer, and the organization teased a "major event" coming in August 2027

City Manager Langloss emphasized that Dixon's combined tourism/Main Street/Chamber model is drawing interest from communities across the state trying to replicate it.

## Dixon Historic Theatre Update

Reed Mitchell, newly appointed board treasurer and a CPA by trade, presented the theater's financial update. Key points:

- The theater hosted **36 shows and 60 total events** in 2025 despite a shortened season for renovations
- Event revenue reached **$494,000**, up from $240,000 in 2024 and $143,000 in 2023
- Concessions brought in roughly $80,000-$85,000 with only about $20,000 in expenses
- The theater is requesting **$200,000 from the city for FY27**, down from $250,000 this year and $350,000 the year before, with plans to continue stepping down by $25,000 annually
- **40 shows** are already booked for 2026

Langloss offered a note of fiscal reality: the Community Development Fund that supports the theater has a $900,000 balance with competing demands, including $200,000 earmarked for housing revitalization. He said staff is satisfied with the theater's financial direction but cautioned that continued high funding levels will shorten the fund's lifespan.

## Public Comment

**Ryan Latvaitis** of Chamberlin Street addressed the council about graffiti on the Berlin Wall monument in Freedom Park. Someone had spray-painted "Revolt" and "Give me liberty or give me death" on the wall, and the city painted over it. Latvaitis argued the monument is dedicated to free expression and urged the council to leave the wall in its current messy state as a reflection of the times. Langloss acknowledged the city covered more than intended but said profane additions couldn't remain.

**Brian Schroeder**, a seventh-grader working toward Eagle Scout, introduced himself as part of a communications merit badge requirement. The council welcomed him warmly.

## Claims and Minutes

The council approved minutes from the March 16 meeting and total claims of **$916,811.64**, which included a $96,969 Illinois EPA loan payment and a $183,333 wastewater treatment project payment.

## Wastewater Treatment Report

Josh McNitt reported that the department maintains roughly 70 miles of sewer main, seven lift stations, and the main treatment plant. The department cleans about 7 miles of sewer per year and treats 40,000 feet for roots annually. Construction on the **$22-23 million treatment plant upgrade** — which received approximately **$8 million in EPA loan forgiveness** — has begun, with a target completion of December 2027. The upgrade is driven by a 2030 phosphorus limit mandate and aging equipment. Councilman Oros asked about stormwater infiltration into the sewer system; McNitt acknowledged it's an ongoing challenge with old, cracked pipes but said the department seals problems as they're found.

## City Manager's Report

Langloss covered several items:

- **Emergency management**: A tornado narrowly missed Dixon the previous week. Chief Ryan Bivins served as interim Emergency Management Director and handled siren decisions. Langloss reminded residents that when sirens sound, they should seek shelter immediately.
- **Grants from Congressman LaHood**: The police department received a **$400,000 grant** for radio systems. The city previously received **$2 million for the Bloody Gulch detention/retention project**. On the day of the meeting, word came of a **$1.4 million grant for Dixon Family YMCA renovations**, with the city as primary recipient and the Y as sub-recipient. Additional pending grants include $1.7-1.8 million for culvert projects and $700,000 for a seawall.
- **Development**: Tropical Smoothie is expected to open by month's end. Another unannounced project is in the works. Ted Hvarre has broken ground at **Pirups Landing**, which will add 72 new homes over four to five years. Work continues on a 55-and-older housing development. Combined, these projects would add more than 100 new addresses to Dixon.
- The **5th Annual Midwest Summit on Leadership** is set for May 21 at the Dixon Historic Theatre, expected to draw 500-plus attendees.

## Council Reports

- **Mayor Hughes** reminded the public of a "Hands Around the Courthouse" event on April 10 at noon at the old Lee County Courthouse in support of child abuse awareness.
- **Councilman Bishop** recognized two retiring longtime businesses: **Bill and Dick's** barbershop (Dick Durr, more than 60 years downtown) and **Oliver's** grocery store, calling both pillars of the community.
- **Councilman Oros** praised the city's grant-writing culture, citing Chief Bivins as an example of department heads who proactively pursue funding opportunities. He also thanked the public commenter for participating.
- **Councilman Venier** echoed the business retirement tributes, gave a shout-out to **Isaiah Roby** — a Dixon native who won the New York Knicks' G League award for community service — and praised a recent Dixon Municipal Band concert at the theater featuring Grammy-winning saxophonist Tom Scott.

## Ordinances Approved (All Unanimous)

- **Liquor code amendments** for four Discover Dixon events: City Market, Country Concert (September 19), Venetian Night (August 8), and Dement Town Music Fest (May 30)
- **Special use permit** for Minify Self-Storage to build a self-storage facility at 508 Kuehl Road, behind the strip mall near Walmart. The plan commission had already recommended approval.

## Budget Amendments Approved (All Unanimous)

Five internal budget transfers were approved across administration, police, public safety building, public works/cemetery, and wastewater departments — all moving money between existing line items for items like overtime, furnace repairs, utility costs, iPads for cemetery software, and credit card processing fees.

## Other Actions (All Unanimous)

- **Street closures** approved for the full slate of 2026 Discover Dixon events, plus the Dixon High School Homecoming Dance and a Rock River Garden Club event
- **Road use agreement** with Meadow Solar LLC for truck traffic related to a solar array being installed at the Dixon Park District facility on Washington Avenue — approximately 30 semi loads, mostly at the front end of the project, with completion expected before summer's end
- **Resolution opposing state preemption of local zoning authority**: Mayor Hughes introduced a resolution pushing back against Governor Pritzker's proposed "build plan" legislation that would limit or remove municipal control over zoning, building codes, parking requirements, and accessory dwelling units. Hughes said he doesn't disagree with everything in the proposals but believes municipalities should control their own planning. Copies will be sent to the Illinois Municipal League, Senator Arilano, and Representative Fritz.

The meeting adjourned after completing all agenda items.
