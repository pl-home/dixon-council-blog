# Dixon City Council Meeting Summary — May 18, 2026

## New Police Officers Sworn In

Two new officers took their oaths of office. **Jack Dempsey**, a Dixon native and graduate of Sauk Valley Community College and the Sauk Valley Police Academy (where he earned top shot honors), was sworn in first. **Cameron Miller**, a Byron native who holds a bachelor's degree from Augustana College, followed. Both started with the department in late 2025 and are currently in field training.

## Dixon High School Scholastic Bowl Team Honored

Coach Padilla brought several members of the Dixon High School scholastic bowl team to be recognized. The team has had a standout run: winning a tournament in Peoria that earned them a trip to Washington, D.C., beating powerhouse Auburn at sectionals, finishing fourth at the Masonic state tournament, and winning back-to-back regionals. Captain Ronan Quick and Nathan (both named all-sectional and all-conference) and Sebastian Seibel were present. The team is set to compete on the Stateline Quiz Bowl television program against Keith Country Day. Coach Padilla also recognized absent members Jayden Thoms, James Kocar, Gage Forster, Jeremiah Fry, Aaron Fitzanko, and Brady Rosinski.

## Public Comment: Two Residents Air Grievances

**Stephen Rockwood**, a Vietnam veteran and 50-year Dixon resident, delivered a lengthy statement alleging harassment by the Dixon Police Department stemming from a years-old incident, and claiming the Fire Department caused unnecessary damage to his home during a fire response and botched the fire investigation. He said the failed investigation has blocked him from pursuing a product liability lawsuit and put the city on notice of potential legal action against both departments.

**Tyler Shaw**, a lifelong resident, described what he characterized as a mishandled assault case from August 2025, alleging police incompetence in the investigation and claiming arrests were only made after he publicized the matter on social media. He accused the department of systemic failures.

Councilman **Dennis Considine** reminded the public and council afterward that standing policy is not to respond to public comments during the meeting, noting the protocol had slipped briefly during the session.

## Claims and Payments

The council approved $1,372,053.82 in labor and outside claims. Mayor **Glen Hughes** noted that $493,984 goes toward the wastewater treatment plant improvement project and $270,523 toward the fuel station project.

## Staff Presentations

**City Hall Support Staff** — Michelle (leading the City Hall front office team) presented on the work her small team handles, from utility billing for over 5,900 accounts to the sidewalk program, UTV renewals (72 issued in two months), electrical contractor renewals, and the pool fill program. She noted that staff member Anna facilitated the demolition of five homes under the Strong Communities grant. New hire Kayla Powell joined at the end of February.

**Chief People Officer Danelle** — Approaching her one-year anniversary, Danelle reported on a range of employee wellness and engagement initiatives: anniversary lunches, a quarterly employee newsletter, a 12-member wellness committee, pension education sessions for IMRF and 457 plan participants, a potential partnership with Path to Heal for the employee assistance program, a YMCA membership discount, financial literacy sessions through Sauk Valley Pink, yoga sessions, and exploration of a 529 college savings plan option. A new HR platform is expected to go live for open enrollment in November, with a performance review process planned for fall rollout.

## City Manager's Report

City Manager **Danny Langloss Jr.** praised the City Hall team and Danelle's contributions. He noted the city received 110 applications for a public works position and over 40 for the chief people officer role, calling the city's workplace culture a competitive advantage.

## The Bridge Has a Name: Petunia Crossing

Mayor Hughes walked through the process of naming the new multipurpose pedestrian bridge over the Rock River. After Councilman **Chris Bishop** initially proposed "Liberty Bridge" (with Veterans Memorial Commission backing), and resident Spencer Thompson suggested "Petunia Crossing," the city opened the process to the public. An ad hoc committee — Tom Shaw, Tom Demmer, Jeremy England, Matt Heckman, Amanda Wike, and the mayor — reviewed roughly 500 submissions and narrowed the field to four: Liberty Crossing, Petunia Crossing, Castle View Crossing, and Founders Crossing.

A public vote drew 1,279 responses. **Petunia Crossing** won with 539 votes, followed by Castle View Crossing at 430. The council unanimously approved the name. The bridge is expected to open in early June.

## Council Reports

- **Councilman Bishop** reminded residents of Memorial Day activities: a wreath-laying at 9:30 a.m. by the river, a 10:00 a.m. parade from City Hall to Oakwood Cemetery, and open houses at the VFW and American Legion.

- **Councilman Mike Venier** raised continued concerns about e-bike safety downtown and cited national statistics (233 deaths, 360,000 ER visits between 2017 and 2022). City Attorney **Robert LeSage** reported that the Illinois Senate has unanimously passed e-bike legislation now pending in the House that would preempt local authority and impose statewide age, speed, and licensing requirements. Councilman Considine stressed the city should still be actively warning the public about the dangers. Venier also highlighted local artist **Nora Ruder**, whose illustrations appear in the book *This Is My First Trip to the Bleachers* by Michael Cerami (over 5,000 copies sold). A new mural led by Ruder is starting this week on the Water Department building on River Street.

- Mayor Hughes recognized Dixon High School's students of the month for the year and student of the year **Danny Lovett**, who also qualified for state in the 3,200-meter race.

## Budget Amendments (All Approved Unanimously)

Several housekeeping budget amendments passed:
- **Building & Zoning**: $4,200 shifted from professional development to full-time salary to cover staff transition overlap.
- **Council Legal**: $20,000 moved from property/liability to the city legal line.
- **Library**: $6,000 moved from salary to pension following an employee retirement.
- **Wastewater**: $4,750 shifted from medical insurance to cover higher credit card processing fees and lab testing costs.
- **Water**: $4,500 shifted from salary to contractual for credit card fees and lead/copper testing.

## Financial and Administrative Transitions

With Finance Director **Becky Leslie** and Assistant City Manager **Matt Heckman** both departing, the council approved:
- Transferring IMRF authorized agent status from Leslie to Danelle, effective June 2.
- Updating the city banking resolution to name **Heather McCarter** and **Tim Shipman** alongside City Clerk **Keesha Kunde**, effective June 2.

## Fire and Police Commission Reappointment

**Andrea Cook** was unanimously reappointed to the Board of Fire and Police Commissioners, continuing the term she assumed after Bruce Keller's departure.

## Well 9 Water Tower Painting — $380,400

The council awarded the painting of the elevated storage tank behind Culver's to **Nauman Company Contractors** for $380,400. Eight bids were received (ranging from $294,000 to $688,000; engineer's estimate was $390,000). Three lower bidders were rejected: one couldn't demonstrate sufficient experience, one failed to submit required documents, and one couldn't comply with the Illinois Preference Act. Nauman, a Wisconsin-based union shop with Midwest tank-painting experience, was vetted by Willett Hofmann & Associates. Verizon equipment on the tower will be temporarily removed. Work is expected to begin in fall 2026.

## Petunia Festival Preparations

The council approved the standard liquor code amendment and road closures for the 2026 Petunia Festival (July 2–5, with setup beginning July 1). Closures include portions of Lincoln Statue Drive, Page Park Drive, River Street, South Galena Avenue turn lanes, South Hennepin Avenue, and various blocks for the car show, family night, and parade.

## Donations Approved

- **Dixon Historic Theater**: $200,000 (4-1, with Councilman Bishop voting no). City Manager Langloss noted the theater's finances are moving in the right direction under oversight from Lee County Finance Director Reid, though the city's annual contribution may take a few more years to decrease. Bishop acknowledged the theater is well-run but said the funding issue is a matter of principle for him.
- **WAC CEO Program**: $5,000 (unanimous), same as last year.
- **Sauk Valley Community College IMPACT Program**: $25,000 (unanimous).

All three recipients submitted required financial reports.

## Police Vehicle Purchases

- **2026 Polaris Ranger Crew XP1000**: Approved (3-0, with Considine and Venier abstaining) for $40,157.18, purchased from Full Throttle Powersports in Lena, IL, with upfit from Carl Emergency Vehicles. Came in nearly $15,000 under budget.
- **Two 2026 Chevrolet Tahoes** (command vehicles): Approved for up to $138,676.04 from Carl Emergency Vehicles. Chief **Ryan Bivins** explained the department currently lacks proper command-and-control vehicles. The specialized wiring and equipment installation could not be sourced locally within the needed timeframe, though the city has discussed with local dealer Ken Nelson how to accommodate future orders with more lead time.

## TIF Reimbursement

The council approved a reimbursement request from **Dixon Lodging LLC** under the existing TIF redevelopment agreement. City Attorney LeSage confirmed all invoices and canceled checks were verified.

## New Building Department Software

The council approved a service agreement with **I-Work Systems** for building department permitting and records management, replacing a seven-year-old system. The new platform costs approximately $20,000 annually. Building Official **Tim Shipman** said staff visited Sterling's city offices to see the system in use and came away satisfied. The platform may also benefit the city clerk's office and public works for tracking service requests.

## Street Resurfacing — $1.78 Million

The 2026 street resurfacing project was awarded to **Martin and Company** for $1,777,083.47 — roughly $223,000 under the $2.2 million budget. The project covers milling and paving of approximately 51 city blocks, 6,000 square feet of new sidewalk, reconstruction of downtown brick crosswalks (to be replaced with a stamped pattern), drainage improvements, and crack sealing. A street list is expected within the week.

## Sewage Pump Replacement

The council waived formal bidding and approved purchase of a **Hayward-Gordon sewage pump** for $41,518 for the Lowell Park lift station, replacing a 15-year-old unit. The old pump will be refurbished and kept as a spare. Public Works Director Heckman explained that alternative pumps would require costly plumbing and rail system modifications, making the direct replacement the most practical choice.

The meeting adjourned after approximately two hours.
