# Dixon City Council Meeting Summary — March 17, 2026

**Meeting called to order at 5:30 p.m.** Present: Mayor Glen Hughes, Councilmen Chris Bishop, Dennis Considine, and Mary Oros. Councilman Mike Venier was absent. Invocation by Pastor Nikkel Johnson of First Presbyterian Church.

---

## Public Hearing: Sauk Valley Sewer Connection

Matt Hanson of Willett Hofmann & Associates presented the final planning step for connecting Sauk Valley Community College to Dixon's sanitary sewer system. The college's aging wastewater treatment plant — built when the campus was constructed, with corroding steel structures and failing equipment — needs to be replaced.

The plan: build a new lift station at the college and run roughly 17,500 feet of force main along Route 2 to connect into the existing city sewer near the old Brandywine restaurant area and the Castellon subdivision.

**Cost:** About $2.9 million total. Building a new standalone treatment plant would have run $4.1 million. Through the IEPA revolving loan program, the city expects to secure approximately $2.1 million in principal forgiveness, dropping annual loan payments from $180,000 down to about $46,000.

**Key point for Dixon ratepayers:** The project will have zero cost impact on existing Dixon customers. Sauk Valley Community College will pay all costs — operations, maintenance, and debt service.

Environmental reviews are largely complete, though the state is still reviewing a portion that touches a floodplain. The Miami Tribe of Oklahoma has requested archaeological monitoring in areas near burial sites. If funding is approved by June, construction could start by March 2027 and wrap up by October 2027.

No public comments were received. The hearing closed at 5:43 p.m.

---

## Budget Work Session: FY2026-27 Budget and Theater Funding

The council addressed a pending request from the Dixon Historic Theater. The theater had been asked to provide financial information to the city but had not yet done so. City Manager Danny Langloss said staff plans to meet with the theater's board later this week.

The council agreed to include a **$200,000 placeholder** in the budget for the theater, with the understanding that no money will be released until the theater presents its financials, the council reviews them, and a specific amount is formally approved. This compromise allows the budget process to move forward without delay.

The FY2026-27 budget was placed on file. A public hearing is scheduled for April 6, with final approval set for April 20.

---

## Public Comment: Liquor License Question

Ryan Marshall, owner of Bar Shinny's, told the council a couple wants to rent the commercial kitchen in his establishment to operate a restaurant. The problem: Bar Shinny's current bar-designated liquor license prohibits minors from entering, which limits the restaurant's potential customer base.

City Attorney Robert LeSage acknowledged the issue and said a previously considered liquor code amendment would have addressed it, but that effort was set aside. Mayor Hughes, LeSage, Marshall, and City Clerk Keesha Kunde plan to meet next week to work through the specifics, with the goal of having something ready for council action at the first April meeting.

---

## Claims and Financials

The council approved **$1,332,970.13** in labor and outside claims. Notable payments included:
- $171,100 to the IEPA for a drinking water project loan
- $86,852 as a final payment for general street maintenance
- $632,036 as the city's portion of a RAISE grant (an additional grant has been approved to reimburse this amount, but the reimbursement agreement isn't finalized yet)

Year-to-date financials through February 2026 were accepted without discussion.

---

## Public Works Report: New Director, Big Savings

Matt Huyett, who is succeeding the departing Matt Heckman as Public Works Director, delivered a detailed presentation.

**In-house work savings:** By using city crews for capital projects rather than contractors, Dixon has saved approximately **$3.8 million over five years**. Per-block savings: about $90,000–$100,000 for water main installation, $3,300 for sewer cleaning, and $23,000 for sidewalk work. Huyett said consultants have told him few communities do this level of in-house capital work.

**Street conditions improving:** In 2017, 63% of Dixon's streets were rated fair, poor, or very poor, with only 18% in good-to-excellent condition. By 2025, the very good-to-excellent category has more than doubled to 46%, while fair-or-worse dropped to 41%. The city has resurfaced roughly 40% of its roads over eight years.

**This year's program:** $2.2 million budgeted for street resurfacing, drainage work, $100,000–$150,000 in sidewalk work, and — for the first time — contracted crack sealing, which the city is treating as a pilot program. Crack sealing can extend pavement life by three to seven years.

Huyett praised Heckman's leadership and the street department's work clearing roads after the previous night's snowstorm.

---

## Budget Resolutions (Internal Fund Transfers)

The council approved four budget resolutions, all involving moving money between existing line items — no new funds:

- **Council:** $1,000 moved from professional development to cover increased background check costs due to a surge in solicitor permit applications.
- **Fire Department:** $48,000 moved from EV fund equipment into fire overtime (driven by two members attending paramedic school in Rockford and increased call volume). Also $9,200 for a new "hazard house" educational tool for elementary school fire safety training; the Firefighters Charitable Association is covering half.
- **Police Department:** $18,000 moved from the police social worker line — $17,000 to overtime and $1,000 to the community assistance fund. Chief Ryan Bivins noted the department is effectively down six officers due to academy training, field training, and an injury, though the 31st officer is about to be hired.
- **Library:** Funds moved between maintenance supplies and part-time wages, previously approved by the library board.

---

## Motor Fuel Tax and Street Resurfacing

The council approved the annual IDOT-required resolution appropriating **$700,000** from motor fuel tax funds for street resurfacing.

A professional services agreement with **Willett Hofmann & Associates** was approved for design and construction engineering on this year's general maintenance street resurfacing program, at a total not to exceed **$204,915.37**.

---

## Property Donation Accepted

Building Official Tim Shipman asked the council to accept a donated property at **117 East 4th Street** — a vacant house the city has been working to address for some time. The property owner passed away, and the estate lacks the means to demolish or rehabilitate the structure. The council approved moving forward, though due diligence on clear title remains. Shipman noted the lot is conforming and could eventually be redeveloped, similar to a property the city accepted on West 2nd Street about a year ago.

---

## Other Notes

- **Discover Dixon** will present its annual update at the next council meeting (originally scheduled for this meeting but postponed due to conflicts).
- Councilman Bishop took humorous responsibility for the late-season snowstorm, having put his snowblower away prematurely.
- The scheduled executive session was cancelled.
- Finance Director Becky Leslie is departing; Heather McCarter is stepping into a larger role. City Manager Langloss praised the succession planning that has developed staff like McCarter and Huyett.

**Meeting adjourned.** Next council meeting: April 6, 2026, which will include a public hearing on the FY2026-27 budget.
